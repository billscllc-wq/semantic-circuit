Two things, done right

Content Design

Content design decides what information people need, how it's structured, and how it reads—pairing information architecture with language so people can find it, understand it, and act. It begins with evidence of what users are trying to do, not what an organization wants to say.

Copywriting is part of it, not the whole. Writing the words well matters—and so does deciding what should exist, how it's structured, and how it connects. The structure is the design.

What I bring: two decades and a magazine editor's eye—turning tangled, enterprise-scale content into clear, structured systems that teams can actually maintain.

UX Strategy

An experience is the sum of every decision a team makes. UX strategy gives those decisions a shared vision to aim at—a vivid picture of the experience you intend to create—so thousands of small choices add up to something deliberate instead of accidental.

A strategy is a set of guiding principles that, once adopted, produce a consistent pattern of decisions. It connects business goals to the experience people actually have, and keeps every team moving toward the same intent.

What I bring: a systems thinker who connects business goals to the experience—building information architecture and UX practices from the ground up, and aligning cross-functional teams around one vision.

Credo

01

Prioritize user needs.

Content architecture should reflect what people are looking for, not what the organization wants them to know. User intent drives hierarchy.

02

Surface the content.

Users don't scroll to discover. They navigate to find. Semantically hierarchical pages beat linear scrolls because findability beats aesthetics.

03

Context is meaning.

The same word means different things in different places. Content doesn't carry meaning on its own; it takes it from what surrounds it. Design the context and you design the meaning.

04

Focus on structure before style.

Decide what content is before deciding how it looks. Content models and taxonomy come first; visual design follows the structure, not the other way around.

05

Name things precisely.

A label is the smallest unit of usability. Clear, consistent naming is the difference between findable and lost, so taxonomy is never an afterthought.

06

Edit to the essence.

Every word earns its place or leaves. Clarity is a product of subtraction, and the last edit is usually a cut.

07

Write once, reuse everywhere.

Structured, componentized content scales. Model it well and a single source can serve many surfaces without drifting out of sync.

Bill is Founder and Principal of Semantic Circuit. A content designer and UX strategist, he has spent more than twenty years making complex systems, processes, and experiences clear. He began as a writer and editor, then built and led content and experience across enterprise and agency work—shaping the information architecture, the content systems, and the teams behind them.

The Semantic Circuit is the third of the eight circuits of consciousness. The time-binding semantic circuit is the layer of the mind that runs on symbols and language: the human capacity to encode and carry meaning across generations, building on each idea. Content design is human semantic memory engineering, and it is work in the third circuit. It makes meaning structured, durable, and clear enough to pass forward.
